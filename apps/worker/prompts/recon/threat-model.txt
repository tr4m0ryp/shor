<method>
1. **Establish system context.** One tight paragraph: what the application is, its core trust model, and the deployment shape (proxy, tenancy, auth). Ground it in the deliverables.
2. **Inventory assets.** What is worth protecting (data, capabilities, money, trust)? Assign each a sensitivity: `low | medium | high | critical`. Crown jewels (critical/high) drive prioritization downstream.
3. **Map entry points and trust boundaries.** For each network-reachable entry point, name the trust boundary it crosses (e.g. `internet -> app`, `user -> admin`, `tenant A -> tenant B`) and which assets become reachable past it.
4. **Enumerate threats — at patch-surviving abstraction.** For each, decide:
   - `actor`: one of `remote_unauth | remote_auth | adjacent_network | local_user | local_admin | supply_chain | insider`.
   - `impact`: `low | medium | high | critical | existential`.
   - `likelihood`: `very_rare | rare | unlikely | possible | likely | very_likely | almost_certain`. **Evidence RAISES likelihood** — a concrete source/recon pointer that the abuse case is reachable and under-defended should push likelihood up; a purely hypothetical concern stays low.
   - `controls`: the defenses that currently stand in the way (and whether they look sufficient).
   - `evidence`: the concrete recon/source pointer(s) grounding this threat (`file:line`, an endpoint + observed behaviour, a manifest entry). Empty only if genuinely none.
   - `status`: short state, e.g. `open`, `needs_verification`, `mitigated`.
   Prioritize by impact x likelihood. The top threats are what the category agents should chase first.
5. **Deprioritize honestly.** List candidate threats you considered and set aside, each with a one-line reason (out of scope, strong control in place, negligible impact). This prevents downstream agents from re-litigating dead ends.
6. **Record provenance.** Which deliverables you drew from, and any gaps/assumptions.
</method>
